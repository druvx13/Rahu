Opening Verse

That which is known through the Mahopanishad, established as the conscious space,

The supreme non-dual empire—that Rama Brahma is my refuge.

Invocation

Om, may my limbs, speech, vital force, eyes, ears, strength, and all senses be nourished.

All is the Brahman of the Upanishads. May I not reject Brahman, and may Brahman not reject me.

Let there be no rejection, let there be no rejection for me.

May the virtues in the Upanishads reside in me, who am devoted to the self.

May they reside in me.

Peace Chant

Om, peace, peace, peace.

Chapter 1

Now, we shall explain the Mahopanishad. It is said: In the beginning, there was only Narayana.

There was no Brahma, no Ishana, no waters, no fire and moon, no heaven and earth,

No stars, no sun, no moon. He, being alone, did not enjoy. From his meditation, the sacrifice was born.

From that, fourteen Purushas were born: one maiden, ten senses, the mind as the eleventh,

Tejas as the twelfth, ego as the thirteenth, breath as the fourteenth, the self as the fifteenth,

And intellect as the sixteenth. The five elements, five subtle elements, and five gross elements.

Thus, the one becomes the twenty-five Purushas. Having placed the Purusha, the years are born.

From the years, others are born. Then again, Narayana desired another through meditation.

From his meditation, from his forehead, a three-eyed, trident-holding Purusha was born,

Bearing prosperity, fame, truth, celibacy, austerity, detachment, mind, sovereignty,

With the Pranava, Vyāhṛtis, Rig, Yajur, Sama, Atharva Vedas, all meters resting in his limbs.

Therefore, he is Ishana, the great god, Mahadeva. Then again, Narayana desired another through meditation.

From his meditation, sweat fell from his forehead. These became the spread-out waters.

Then, a golden orb of light appeared. In it, the four-faced Brahma was born. He meditated:

Facing east, "Bhūḥ" as the Vyāhṛti, Gāyatrī meter, Rig Veda, Agni as the deity.

Facing west, "Bhuvaḥ" as the Vyāhṛti, Triṣṭubh meter, Yajur Veda, Vāyu as the deity.

Facing north, "Svaḥ" as the Vyāhṛti, Jagatī meter, Sama Veda, Surya as the deity.

Facing south, "Mahaḥ" as the Vyāhṛti, Anuṣṭubh meter, Atharva Veda, Soma as the deity.

The god with a thousand heads, a thousand eyes, the source of the universe,

The eternal supreme beyond the universe, the universe is Narayana, Hari.

This universe is indeed the Purusha, and the universe lives on him.

The lord of the universe, the god in the ocean, having the form of the universe,

Like a lotus bud, resembling a hanging sheath, with the heart facing downward,

For the sake of continuity, with hissing sounds. In its midst is a great flame,

Universal flame, facing all directions. In the middle of that is the flame of fire,

Smaller than the smallest, standing upward. In the middle of that flame, the supreme self is established.

He is Brahma, he is Ishana, he is Indra, he is the imperishable, the supreme ruler. Thus is the Mahopanishad.

End of the First Chapter.

Chapter 2

There was a sage named Shuka, of great splendor, devoted to the bliss of his own nature.

From birth, he, the king of sages, attained the truth.

Through his own discrimination, this great-minded one,

After long and proper reflection, attained certainty about the self.

Because it is nameless and unreachable, situated beyond the mind and the six senses,

It is pure consciousness, the atomic self, subtler than space.

Within the supreme conscious atom, the dust particles of billions of universes

Undergo creation and sustenance and dissolve through the cycle of power.

Space is external emptiness, but due to consciousness, it is not space.

There is nothing that is indescribable, yet it is called existence.

It is conscious because it is luminous, and due to the absence of knowables, it is like a stone.

In its own self, in the space, it creates the wonderful manifestation of the world.

This universe is merely its light; it cannot be separate from it.

Even the diversity of the world is its light; thus, diversity is also made of it.

It is all-pervading, related to all, yet due to the absence of movement, it does not go.

It does not exist due to the absence of support, yet it exists due to its nature of existence.

It is the bliss of knowledge, Brahman, the refuge of the giver and the receiver.

The renunciation of all resolves and the acceptance by the mind.

The wise call it the absence of perception in the waking state, yet it is perception.

By its contraction and expansion, the dissolution and creation of the world occur.

It is the culmination of Vedanta statements, beyond the reach of words.

I am indeed the Brahman of existence, consciousness, and supreme bliss, not anything else.

Through his own subtle intellect, Shuka knew everything.

Having attained the supreme reality, his mind rested without agitation.

He did not trust in the reality of this world in relation to the self.

His mind simply ceased its restlessness towards objects,

Like a bird turning away from abundant, breaking streams.

Once, this pure-minded one, seated alone on Mount Meru,

Asked his father, the sage Krishna Dvaipayana (Vyasa), with devotion:

"O sage, how did this spectacle of the world arise?

How does it attain peace? What, whose, when—tell me."

Thus asked, the sage Vyasa told his son everything properly,

As it should be spoken by one who knows the self.

"I did not know this before," said Shuka, but his father replied,

With his own intellect, Shuka did not regard the words highly.

Vyasa, the blessed one, understanding his son’s intention,

Said again to his son, "I do not know it truly.

There is a king named Janaka in the city of Mithila.

He knows what is to be known properly; from him, you will attain everything."

Thus told by his father, Shuka went from Mount Meru to the earth,

To the city of Videha, ruled by Janaka.

He was announced to the great-souled Janaka by the gatekeepers:

"O king, here stands Shuka, the son of Vyasa, at the gate."

To test Shuka, Janaka said with indifference, "Let him be,"

And remained silent for seven days.

Then, Janaka had Shuka brought into the courtyard.

There, he stayed for seven days, undisturbed.

Then, Janaka brought him into the inner court.

"The king is not to be seen," and thus he stayed for seven days.

There, with intoxicated beautiful women, food, and heaps of pleasures,

Janaka pampered Shuka, whose face was like the moon.

But those pleasures and foods did not attract the mind of Vyasa’s son,

Like a gentle breeze does not move a firmly fixed mountain.

Only serene, pure, silent, with a joyful mind,

Like the full moon, the spotless Shuka stood.

Having recognized his nature, King Janaka brought him,

And seeing him with a joyful self, bowed to him.

Having accomplished all duties in the world, having attained all desires,

He said, "What do you desire?" after welcoming him.

"How did this spectacle of the world arise, O guru?

How does it attain peace? Tell me properly and quickly."

Everything was properly told by the great-souled Janaka,

Just as it was previously told by his father, the great-minded one.

"I myself had already known this specially.

Indeed, this was told by my father when asked."

"This very meaning has been told by you, O best of speakers.

This very meaning is seen in the scriptures."

"It is born from the mind’s imaginations, and with the destruction of imaginations,

It perishes; the burnt world is essenceless, it is certain."

"Then what is this, O great one? Tell me the truth, the immovable.

From you, I attain rest with my mind wandering in the world."

"Listen now to what is being said by me,

O Shuka, the expansion of knowledge, the essence of intellect within.

Through whose knowledge, a person immediately attains the state of living liberation.

The cleansing of the mind from the seen, with the understanding that the seen does not exist,

When accomplished, then the supreme peace of nirvana arises.

The complete abandonment of desires is called liberation by the good;

That alone is the pure path.

Those with pure desires do not again become partakers of birth and misfortune.

They are called knowers of what is to be known, the living liberated ones with great intellect.

The firmness in the contemplation of objects is called bondage.

The attenuation of desires, O Brahman, is called liberation.

For whom, without any cause like austerity,

Pleasures here do not appeal, he is called the living liberated.

In the face of pleasures and pains that come in due time,

He who neither rejoices nor grieves is called the living liberated.

He who is not touched within by joy, anger, fear, wrath, desire, or miserliness,

Is called the living liberated.

He who, having abandoned the desire born of ego,

Stays playfully, abandoning the object of meditation, is called the living liberated.

He who has no praise or blame for desired or undesired things in his inner vision,

And moves like one in deep sleep, is called the living liberated.

Seated in spiritual delight, complete, with a pure mind,

Having attained the supreme rest, he desires nothing here.

He who lives without attachment is called the living liberated.

He whose consciousness is not tainted even a little by the knowable in the space of the heart,

That non-inert consciousness is called the living liberated.

He who performs actions without regard for attachment, aversion, happiness, sorrow, virtue, vice, fruit, or non-fruit,

Is called the living liberated.

He who is silent, without ego, without pride, free from envy,

Who acts without agitation, is called the living liberated.

He who, without attachment everywhere, stays like a witness,

Desireless in actions, is called the living liberated.

He who has completely abandoned within virtue, vice, mind, and desires,

Is called the living liberated.

He who has completely abandoned the entire imagination of the seen that is perceived,

Is called the living liberated.

He who eats pungent, sour, salty, bitter, uncooked, and cooked food equally,

Is called the living liberated.

He who enjoys old age, death, adversity, kingdom, and poverty as delightful,

Is called the living liberated.

He who has completely abandoned with intellect virtue, vice, happiness, sorrow, death, and birth,

Is called the living liberated.

He who, free from agitation and joy, with a pure intellect,

Neither grieves nor rejoices, is called the living liberated.

He who has abandoned with intellect all desires, all doubts, all efforts, all certainties,

Is called the living liberated.

He whose mind is equal in birth, existence, destruction, rising, and setting,

Is called the living liberated.

He who hates nothing and desires nothing,

Who enjoys natural pleasures, is called the living liberated.

He who, though having parts, is without parts, though having a mind, is without a mind,

With the calculations of the world calmed, is called the living liberated.

He who, though engaged in all affairs, is desireless,

Like one who is complete in others’ affairs, is called the living liberated.

Having abandoned the state of living liberation, when the body is claimed by time,

He enters the state of disembodied liberation, like the wind becoming still.

The disembodied liberated one neither rises nor sets, neither calms down,

Neither is nor is not, neither near nor far, neither I nor another.

Then, something remains, still, profound, neither light nor darkness spread out,

Unnamed, unmanifested, existing.

Neither void nor form, neither seen nor seeing,

Nor the multitude of elemental objects existing infinitely.

Something indescribable, fuller than the full,

Neither being nor non-being, neither existence nor non-existence, neither emotion nor imagination.

Pure consciousness without objects, infinite, ageless, auspicious,

Without beginning, middle, or end, beginningless, free from disease.

That which is remembered as the vision in the midst of the seer, seeing, and seen,

There is no higher certainty than this, O sage.

You yourself have known it, and again heard it from the guru.

Bound by one’s own resolve, one is liberated by the absence of resolve.

Thus, you yourself have known what is to be known by the great-souled one,

From whom aversion to pleasures or the entire seen has arisen.

You have attained all that is to be attained with a complete mind.

In your own nature, in austerity, O Brahman, you are liberated; abandon delusion.

Seeing the extremely external, the external, the internal, and the innermost of the intellect,

O Shuka, though seeing, you do not see; you are the witness, complete and alone.

Shuka rested in silence, established in the supreme reality,

Free from sorrow, fear, and effort, desireless, with doubts cut off.

He went to the peak of Mount Meru for uninterrupted meditation.

There, for thousands of years, in unwavering meditation,

He stayed in that place and became calm, like a lamp without oil in himself.

With all imaginations and impurities gone, pure,

He, the great soul, in his own spotless self, in the sacred state,

Like a drop of water in the ocean, attained oneness, with desires dissolved.

Thus is the Mahopanishad.

End of the Second Chapter.

Chapter 3

There was a sage named Nidagha, who had attained knowledge as a child.

He traveled to sacred places with his father’s permission.

Having bathed in three and a half crore sacred places, he returned home.

The highly renowned one bowed to Ribhu and told his story.

"From the merit of bathing in three and a half crore sacred places,

A thought has arisen in my mind, and it is like this:

The world is born and dies, and dies for rebirth.

All these, with their actions, moving and unmoving, are unstable.

They are the abode of all calamities, sinful states, and places of wealth.

Like iron rods, they are mutually unattached.

Only states dry up through this imagination of the mind.

Attachment to states has come like a traveler in deserts.

How does this suffering calm down? I am tormented in mind.

The cycles of thoughts do not bring me joy; wealth does not.

Houses with offspring are like fierce calamities.

This delicate prosperity in the world is for delusion, O sage; it surely does not give happiness.

Life is fragile like a drop of water hanging on the tip of a leaf.

Like a madman, abandoning the body in the southern direction.

For those whose minds are decayed by attachment to poisonous objects,

Whose self-discrimination is not mature, life is a cause of toil.

It is fitting to wrap the wind, to break space,

To knot waves, but attachment to life is not fitting.

That by which the attainable is attained, by which one does not grieve again,

That is called life, the place of supreme peace.

Even trees live, animals and birds live.

He lives whose mind lives by contemplation.

Only those beings in the world live well

Who are not born again here; the rest are old donkeys.

Scriptures are a burden for the discriminating, knowledge is a burden for the attached.

The mind is a burden for the unpeaceful, the body is a burden for one who does not know the self.

From ego come calamities, from ego come difficulties.

From ego come desires; there is no enemy greater than ego.

Whatever I have enjoyed, moving and unmoving, due to ego,

All that is unreal; the reality is the emptiness of ego.

Here and there, very anxiously, it runs in vain.

The mind goes far, like a dog in a village.

Led to inertness by the cruel one, following the wife of desire,

Controlled like by a dog, O Brahman, I am liberated in mind.

Even from drinking the ocean, from uprooting Mount Sumeru,

From eating fire, O Brahman, the control of the mind is difficult.

The mind is the cause of objects; when it exists, the three worlds exist.

When it is destroyed, the world is destroyed; it should be treated with effort.

Whichever excellence of qualities I resort to, O best of sages,

Desire cuts it off like a mouse cuts a string.

It makes a step even in the impassable, satisfied, it strives in vain.

Desire, like a restless monkey, does not stay in one place for long.

In a moment, it goes to the netherworld, in a moment to the sky,

In a moment, it wanders in the groves of directions, desire, the six-footed one in the heart-lotus.

Among all the sorrows of the world, desire alone gives long sorrow,

Which, even staying in the inner chambers, places one in great difficulty.

The mantra for the cholera of desire is the abandonment of thought, O twice-born.

By a little, it brings joy; by a little, it brings sorrow.

There is no one as pitiable as the body, lowly, devoid of qualities.

The body is the great house of the householder ego.

Whether it rolls or stays firm, what is it to me, O guru?

The body-house, with senses bound in a row, desire prancing in the courtyard,

Filled with mind-servants, is not desired by me.

With the tongue-monkey occupying the mouth-door, terrifying,

With seen fragments of teeth and bones, the body-house is not desired by me.

Of this body made of blood and flesh, both inside and outside, O sage,

Whose only nature is destruction, tell me, what is the beauty of the body?

He who has determined stability in lightning, autumn clouds, and Gandharva cities,

Let him trust in the body.

In childhood, there is fear from the teacher, from the mother, from the father,

From people, from elders and children; childhood is a house of fear.

By the demon of desire dwelling in the cave of one’s own mind, causing various delusions,

One is forcibly overpowered and helpless.

Servants, sons, women, relatives, and friends

Laugh at the man trembling with old age like a madman.

In old age, the long desire full of wretchedness increases,

The sole friend of all calamities, causing burning in the heart.

Wherever this notion of happiness exists in the world,

Time, having obtained it like a pillar, cuts it off.

Grass, dust, Indra, gold, Mount Meru, mustard seed—

With self-conceit, all are ready to be made one’s own.

This time, the destroyer of all, has conquered the three worlds.

Of the woman, a puppet of flesh, with a cage of limbs moving like a machine,

Full of sinews, bones, and knots, what is beautiful?

Having separated the skin, flesh, blood, tears, and water in the eyes,

Look at it; if it is beautiful, why are you deluded in vain?

Like the play of the Ganges on the slopes of Mount Meru’s peak,

Seen in which, O sage, the playfulness of a pearl necklace.

In cremation grounds, at the ends of directions, that same breast of a woman

Is tasted by dogs in time, like a small lump of food.

Women, with hair like collyrium, hard to touch, pleasing to the eyes,

Are flames of the fire of sin, burning men like straw.

Even burning from afar, even if juicy, they are dry.

Women are indeed the fuel for the fires of hell, beautiful yet terrible.

Scattered by the hunter named desire, the bewildered-minded

Women are the snares for the birds of men, binding their limbs.

For men, fish in the pond of birth, moving in the mud of mind,

Woman is the bait on the hook of evil desires.

With this excellent box of all jewels of faults,

With the chain of sorrow, forever enough for me with woman.

He who has a woman has desire for enjoyment; where is the place of enjoyment for one without a woman?

Having abandoned woman, the world is abandoned; having abandoned the world, one becomes happy.

Even directions are not seen, nor is another country a teacher.

Even mountains crumble, even stars decay.

Even oceans dry up, even the pole star has an unstable life.

Even the perfected perish, even the demons age.

Even the supreme lord has an end, even Hari, the unborn, declines.

Even existence becomes non-existence, even the lords of directions age.

Brahma, Vishnu, Rudra, and all beings

Pursue destruction like waters to the ocean.

Calamities come for a moment, prosperity comes for a moment,

Birth for a moment, then death; all is indeed perishable.

Heroes are killed by the unheroic, a hundred are killed by one.

Poison is the inequality of objects; poison is not called poison.

Objects destroy across births; poison destroys one birth.

Thus, in my mind, burnt by the forest fire of faults,

Desires for enjoyment do not shine even in the mirage lake.

Therefore, enlighten me quickly with the knowledge of truth, O guru.

Otherwise, adopting silence, without pride, free from envy,

Contemplating Vishnu with the mind, like one dedicated to writing."

Thus is the Mahopanishad.

End of the Third Chapter.

Chapter 5

"Nidagha, there is nothing else for you to know, O best among the knowers.

With your blessed intellect, you know through grace.

I shall wipe away the confusion born of the impurity of the mind, O sage.

At the door of liberation, four gatekeepers are proclaimed:

Calmness, inquiry, contentment, and the fourth, association with the good.

One should resort to one or all with all effort, abandoning everything.

When one is controlled, all four come under control.

First, for liberation from the world, one should develop wisdom

Through scriptures, contact with good people, austerity, and self-control.

When there is agreement between one’s experience, the scriptures, and the guru,

Through practice, the self is always seen.

If you abandon every moment the pursuit of resolves and desires,

Then you have certainly attained the state of mindlessness, O pure one.

The non-doership of the mind is called concentration.

That alone is the state of aloneness, the supreme peace with auspiciousness.

Having completely abandoned all thoughts of self and others with the mind,

Stay as you are, like a mute, deaf, and blind person.

All is calm, unborn, one, without beginning or middle,

Luminous, self-knowing, without signs of objects.

'All is calm' is a view made of words,

Merely for the sake of refutation; thus, 'Om' is said.

Being always awake in mind, even while performing worldly actions,

Having known the oneness of the self, stay like an undisturbed great ocean.

The understanding of truth alone is the fire for the grass of desires,

Called concentration, not mere silent sitting.

Just as people act in the presence of a desireless gem,

So does this multitude of the world in the mere presence of the supreme truth.

Therefore, O sage, in the self, there is both doership and non-doership.

Due to desirelessness, it is a non-doer; due to mere presence, it is a doer.

Know these two in Brahman, O sage, doership and non-doership.

Wherever this wonder is, relying on that, be steady.

Therefore, with the thought 'I am always a non-doer,' meditate with intellect.

That supreme nectar-named equanimity alone remains.

Nidagha, listen: those established in goodness, born on earth with great virtues,

Are always exalted, joyful like the moon in themselves.

They do not come to grief in calamity, like a golden lotus at night.

They do not deviate from nature, they delight in the path of the good.

They shine with their form, with virtues like friendliness,

Equal, with equal taste, always with good conduct.

Like the ocean, they have deep limits, with clear intentions.

They do not abandon regularity, like the sun, the great ones.

'Who am I? How is this?'—this dirt of the world spread out,

Should be carefully examined by the wise with the good.

One should not be engaged in actions, nor dwell with the ignoble.

The destroyer of all, death, should not be disregarded.

Having abandoned the body made of bones and flesh, unattractive with blood, etc.,

One should see the self as pure consciousness, the thread of liberated elements.

The rejection of the acceptable, the abandonment of the rejectable alone,

That is the form of the mind; know it as external, not otherwise.

Through the path taught by the guru and scriptures, and through one’s experience,

In the dense consciousness, knowing 'I am Brahman,' one becomes a sage free from sorrow.

Where the falling of a sharp sword is endured like striking a lotus,

Burning by fire like sprinkling with snow,

Rolling on embers like applying sandalwood,

The falling of endless arrows like the shower of a summer house,

Cutting off one’s head like pleasant sleep,

Becoming mute like sealing the mouth,

Deafness like a great gain—thus, without disregard,

From firm detachment, knowledge arises.

Through the words of the guru and the purity of one’s experience,

By whose practice the self is always seen.

Just as for one whose confusion about directions is destroyed, directions appear as before,

So, with knowledge, the destroyed world does not exist—thus contemplate.

Neither wealth, nor friends, nor relatives help,

Nor the affliction of the body, nor resorting to sacred places.

Only that, which is merely the mind, attains the state.

The sorrows and desires that are unbearable, the difficulties,

In those with peaceful minds, all that vanishes like darkness in the sun.

Like a mother, both harsh and gentle things

Gain trust in the world in one with calmness.

Not through drinking elixirs, nor through embracing wealth,

Does one attain such happiness as through inner peace.

Having heard, touched, eaten, seen, known good and evil,

He who neither rejoices nor grieves is called peaceful.

He whose mind is clear like the moon in snow, undisturbed,

In death, festivals, and battles, is called peaceful.

Among ascetics, the learned, sacrificers, kings,

The strong, the virtuous, the one with peace shines.

Those who are peaceful, having drunk the nectar of contentment,

Are satisfied; the great-souled ones delighting in the self have attained the great state.

Having abandoned the unattained, being equal in what is attained,

He who has no sorrow or joy in the unseen is called content.

He who does not rejoice in the unattained, enjoys the attained as desired,

With gentle conduct, is called content.

He rejoices in the fortitude attained, like a virtuous woman in the inner court.

That state of living liberation arises, giving the bliss of one’s nature.

At the right time, according to scriptures, according to place, according to comfort,

According to the possible association with the good, this path to liberation,

The wise one should reflect upon until rest in the self.

For one who has attained rest in the fourth state, withdrawn from the ocean of existence,

Whether living or not living, whether a householder or an ascetic,

There is no purpose in what is done or not done, nor in the confusion of scriptures and traditions.

Like the ocean without a churner, he stays as he is.

When the pure knowledge of all selves arises as your own,

It shines beyond space, time, and directions, with the body of consciousness.

Thus, when the self has attained manifestation wherever,

It stays there quickly, and shines in that form.

Whatever is seen, the entire world, moving and unmoving,

Perishes at the end of the age like a dream in deep sleep.

'Truth, self, supreme Brahman, reality,' etc., are imagined by the wise

For the sake of transaction, as names of that great-souled one.

Just as the meaning of 'bracelet' is not separate from gold,

Nor from the golden bracelet, so is the meaning of 'world' supreme.

Therefore, this magic of Indra is spread in the world.

The inner existence of the seer and the seen is called bondage.

The seer is bound by the seen; in the absence of the seen, he is liberated.

The creation like 'I,' 'you,' 'world,' etc., is called the seen.

Through the mind alone, the magic of Indra is spread in the world.

As long as this exists, liberation does not exist.

The world is created by Brahman through the mind alone.

Therefore, the world that is seen is made of mind.

The mind does not exist externally nor in the heart in the form of existence.

That which is the appearance for the sake of meaning is called mind.

Know that imagination is the mind; imagination does not exist.

Where there is imagination, there the mind is understood to be.

When imagination and mind are destroyed, never by anyone,

When imagination is born and dissolved, the self-nature remains.

When 'I,' 'you,' 'world,' etc., are calmed, the confusion of the seen,

Then such aloneness may be; when the seen attains existence,

At the time of the great dissolution, when complete non-existence is attained,

When all seen is completely dissolved, at the beginning of creation, only peace remains.

There is an unsetting, luminous, unborn, divine, free from disease,

Always, all-doing, all, called the supreme self.

From which words turn back, which is attained by the liberated,

Whose names like self, etc., are imagined, not by nature.

The mental space, the conscious space, and the third space,

Know the conscious space as emptier than the two, O great sage.

When the mind goes from one place to another, that which is in between,

In an instant, know that as the conscious space, O best of sages.

If you enter that state where all resolves are abandoned,

You will certainly attain the peaceful state that is all-pervading.

That which is born of generosity, beauty, detachment, and the essence of joy,

Flowing with bliss, is called concentration.

Through the understanding that the seen does not exist, in the attenuation of attachment and aversion,

The delight that arises with strength is called concentration.

The understanding that the seen does not exist is knowledge; the knowable is of the nature of consciousness.

That alone is the state of aloneness; everything else is false.

An elephant is bound in the corner of a mustard seed,

A battle is fought by a mosquito with a multitude of lions in a dust particle.

A lotus-eyed one places Mount Meru, swallowed by a bee’s son.

Know, O Nidagha, that such is this world, made of delusion.

The mind alone is the world, tainted by diseases and afflictions.

When it is free from them, it is called the end of existence.

Through the mind, the body attains embodiment.

Freed from the desire for the body, one is not tainted by bodily qualities.

It makes an age into a moment, a moment into an age.

The play of the mind is the world; this is my certain opinion.

Without abandoning bad conduct, without peace, without concentration,

Or with an unpeaceful mind, one does not attain it through knowledge.

That Brahman, bliss, without duality, without qualities, dense with truth and consciousness,

Having known the form of one’s self, one never fears.

That which is greater than the great, the great one,

Eternal, auspicious, made of the light of its own nature,

The ancient seer, the eternal Purusha,

The lord of all, worshipped by all gods.

'I am Brahman' is certainly the cause of liberation for the great-souled.

There are two words for bondage and liberation: 'mine' and 'not mine.'

By 'mine,' a being is bound; by 'not mine,' he is liberated.

In the form of the individual and the lord, as conscious and inert,

The creation from seeing to entering is imagined by the lord.

The world from waking to liberation is imagined by the individual.

From Triṇācika to Yoga, relying on the delusion of the lord,

From Lokāyata to Sāṃkhya, relying on the delusion of the individual.

Therefore, for those desiring liberation, the mind should not be on the individual or the lord,

But the truth of Brahman should be reflected upon with a steady mind.

He who sees everything without distinction due to the connection with consciousness,

He alone is directly the knower, he is Shiva, he is Hari, he is Brahma.

Difficult is the abandonment of objects, difficult is the vision of truth,

Difficult is the natural state without the grace of the good guru.

For the yogi whose power of knowledge has arisen, whose all actions are destroyed,

The natural state arises by itself.

When indeed this man knows even a little difference in this,

Then he has fear; there is no doubt about it.

He sees with the eye of knowledge the all-pervading existence, consciousness, and bliss.

The eye of ignorance does not see, like the sun for the blind.

Knowledge alone is that Brahman, characterized by truth and knowledge.

Thus, through the complete knowledge of Brahman, a mortal becomes immortal.

The knot of the heart is broken, all doubts are cut,

And his actions are destroyed when that supreme and inferior is seen.

Abandoning the non-self, in the unchanging state of the world,

With single-mindedness, being inward, be devoted to pure consciousness.

In the ocean of the world, all water is the desert itself.

This triple world is all pure consciousness through self-reflection.

Having abandoned the mind that is attached and unattached,

He who stays as the self alone, he is indeed Shiva, the best of knowers of Brahman.

The incomparable basis, beyond speech and mind,

Eternal, all-pervading, omnipresent, extremely subtle, imperishable.

The play of the great lord of all powers is the mind and the world.

Through control and lack of control, the world attains peace.

I shall tell you the remedy for the disease of the mind.

By abandoning whatever is desired, one attains liberation.

The art of creation, which is established, is called attachment.

From the practice of the five stages, firmly delighting in the self,

From the non-contemplation of internal and external objects.

The sixth stage is the contemplation of objects,

Which is the understanding through the effort of others for a long time.

From the long practice of the six stages, from the non-perception of difference,

The state of being established in one’s own nature is to be known as the fourth state.

This indeed exists in the living liberated as the fourth state.

Beyond that is the disembodied liberation.

Those great ones, O Nidagha, who have attained the seventh stage,

Delighting in the self, the great-souled ones have attained the great state.

The living liberated do not immerse in the states of pleasure and pain.

Whether they do or do not do anything according to nature or duty.

Awakened by those nearby, following the previous conduct,

They perform actions like one awakened from sleep.

This sevenfold stage is within the scope of the wise.

Having attained this state of knowledge, even beasts and barbarians,

Whether with body or without body, they are liberated; there is no doubt.

Knowledge is indeed the cutting of the knot; when it is present, there is liberation.

Like the understanding of water in a mirage, it is merely calming.

Those who have crossed the ocean of delusion have attained the supreme state.

They, established in these stages, devoted to attaining the self,

The means of calming the mind is called yoga.

The seven stages are to be known; those stages have been described.

The goal of these stages is the state called Brahman.

Where there is no sense of 'I' or 'you,' no supreme state,

No imagination anywhere, no existence or non-existence in perception.

All is calm, without support, established in space, eternal, auspicious,

Free from disease, without appearance, without name, without cause.

Neither being nor non-being, neither middle nor end, neither all nor everything,

Ungraspable by mind and speech, fuller than the full, happier than happiness.

The non-perception that is completely calm, the perception of the self spread out.

The existence of all objects is nothing but perception.

In the relationship of the seer and the seen, the vision in between,

Free from seer, seeing, and seen, that is this state.

When the mind goes from one place to another, the consciousness in between,

The non-inert perception, be always absorbed in that.

That which is your eternal form, without waking, dream, or sleep,

Neither inert nor non-inert, be always absorbed in that.

Having abandoned the inertness, the heart is like a stone,

Whose nature is without mind, be always absorbed in that.

Having completely abandoned the mind, be steady as you are.

The mind arose from the supreme self-truth,

By it, this world is spread with the net of imaginations.

Like the sky, which is empty, yet appears blue,

O sage, it shines beautifully with the name of blue.

When the mind is dissolved through the destruction of resolves,

The mists of delusion in the world dissolve.

Like the sky in autumn, it shines clearly,

Pure consciousness alone, unborn, primeval, infinite within.

Like a picture arising in the sky without a doer or color,

The experience of the self without seeing, like a dream without sleep.

In the witness, equal, pure, without imaginations, in the self of consciousness,

The worlds reflect desirelessly, like in a mirror.

One Brahman, the space of consciousness, all-pervading, undivided.

Contemplate this with effort for the calming of the mind’s restlessness.

Like a stone with lines upon lines,

So see the one Brahman enveloped by the three worlds.

Due to the absence of a second cause, this world is uncreated.

Known, to be known now, seen, to be seen, wonderful.

I am rested, long tired; there is nothing but pure consciousness.

See, with doubts rested, with all curiosity gone.

The supreme state is without the net of imaginations, without mind.

Those alone have attained the state, with all sins calmed.

The great-minded, with peaceful intellects, who have attained mindlessness.

For a being who has reflected, whose mind’s functions have fallen,

Who always abandons contemplation, abandoning the rejectable, attaining the acceptable.

Seeing the seer always, not seeing the non-seer,

In the supreme truth to be known, being awake while living.

For one asleep in the path of the world, which is a delusion of wealth,

From extreme ripened detachment, even in tastes without taste.

When the net of world-desires is broken like a bird’s net,

When the knot of the heart is loosened by the speed of detachment.

As water becomes clear by obtaining the clearing nut,

So the nature becomes clear through knowledge.

Free from attachment, without association, without duality, without support,

The mind goes out from delusion like a bird from a cage.

With doubts and wickedness calmed, with curiosity and confusion gone,

The mind, full within, shines like the full moon.

There is neither I nor anything else here; I am Brahman, free from disease.

Thus, from the middle of existence and non-existence, he who sees, sees.

As the mind falls without attachment on objects seen by the eyes,

So does the intellect of the wise fall on actions.

When enjoyment is fully known and experienced, it becomes satisfaction.

When a thief is known and served, he becomes a friend, not a thief.

Like travelers who unexpectedly come upon a village festival,

So do the wise see the splendor of enjoyment.

The playful enjoyment of the controlled mind, even if small,

Is considered great due to its difficulty, without expansion.

A king, bound and liberated, is satisfied with a mere morsel.

Unbound by others, unconquered, he does not consider the kingdom much.

Pressing hand with hand, grinding teeth with teeth,

Attacking limbs with limbs, first conquer your own mind.

From the conquest of the mind, there is no other way in the ocean of existence.

In the great hell of the empire, the intoxicated elephants of misdeeds,

Full of arrows of desires, the enemies of the senses are hard to conquer.

For one whose pride of mind is destroyed, whose enemies of the senses are controlled,

The desires for enjoyment diminish like lotuses in winter.

As long as the mind is not conquered through firm practice of the one truth,

The desires dwell in the heart like demons at night.

The servant due to doing what is desired, the minister due to causing all purposes,

The vassal due to conquering the senses—I consider the mind of the discriminating.

From nurturing, the affectionate beloved; from protecting, the protective father;

From excellent arrangement, the friend—I consider the mind of the wise.

From its own light, from the vision of scriptures, from one’s own intellect, from one’s experience,

It gives supreme accomplishment; abandoning oneself, the mind is the father.

Very joyful, very firm, very pure, very accomplished, very awakened,

With its own quality, it shines in the heart, the charming jewel of the mind.

This jewel of the mind, O Brahman, stained with much mud,

Wash it with the water of discrimination for success and become enlightened.

Relying on supreme discrimination, seeing the truth with intellect,

Cutting the enemies of the senses, cross the ocean of existence.

They know that attachment alone is the mine of infinite sorrows.

They know that non-attachment is the abode of happiness everywhere.

This world, bound by the threads of desires, revolves.

It is for evident great sorrow; for happiness, it comes to destruction.

Even the wise, very learned, of good family, great,

Are bound by desire like a lion by a chain.

Having taken up supreme effort and diligence according to scriptures,

Without agitation, who does not attain success?

I am all this world, I am the imperishable supreme self.

There is nothing else—by this understanding, that is the supreme ego.

I am distinct from all, thinner than the tip of a hair.

This is the second auspicious ego, O Brahman.

This is for liberation, not for bondage, for the living liberated.

This body is merely hands, feet, etc.—this is the third certainty,

The worldly ego, which is indeed trivial.

The individual alone is the evil one, the root of the difficult tree of the world.

Struck by this, a being runs lower and lower.

By abandoning this evil ego for a long time,

A being with the remaining ego, peaceful, attains liberation.

Having accepted the first two extraordinary egos,

The third worldly ego, which gives sorrow, should be abandoned.

Then, having abandoned all egos,

He stays very high, ascending to the supreme.

Bondage is merely the desire for enjoyment; its abandonment is called liberation.

The rise of the mind is destruction; the destruction of the mind is great rise.

The mind of the knower attains destruction; the mind is the chain of the ignorant.

Neither joyful nor joyless, neither moving nor still,

Neither being nor non-being, nor their middle—thus know the mind of the knower.

As due to subtlety, the reflection of consciousness in space is not perceived,

So the partless consciousness, though all-pervading, is not perceived.

Devoid of all resolves, devoid of all names,

That consciousness, indestructible, is called the self, etc.

Among the knowers, it is partless, seeing the one self in the pure and impure world.

It neither sets nor rises, neither stands nor stays,

Neither goes nor comes, neither here nor there.

That consciousness is formless, without imaginations, without support.

First, purify the disciple with qualities like calmness and self-control.

Afterwards, teach that all this is Brahman, and you are pure.

He who says to the ignorant or half-awakened, 'All is Brahman,'

Is placed in the nets of great hells.

For one whose intellect is awakened, whose desire for enjoyment is destroyed, who is desireless,

There is no dirt of ignorance—thus should the wise guru instruct.

Like light when there is a lamp, like day when there is the sun,

Like fragrance when there is a flower, so is the world when there is consciousness.

This appears only as an appearance, not in reality.

When the vision of knowledge is clear, when the rise of awakening is spread,

You will know properly, O healthy one, the strength of my words.

Through ignorance alone, with the supreme effort for one’s own destruction,

Knowledge is attained, O Brahman, which removes all faults.

A weapon is calmed by a weapon, dirt is washed by dirt.

Poison goes by poison, an enemy is killed by an enemy.

Such is this elemental illusion, which gives joy through its own destruction.

Its nature is not perceived; when observed, it vanishes.

'It does not exist in reality'—thus one should contemplate with intellect.

That contemplation 'All is Brahman' indeed gives liberation.

The vision of difference is ignorance; abandon it completely.

O sage, that imperishable state is not attained.

Do not have the thought, 'From where did this arise?'

Have the thought, 'How shall I destroy this?'

When it is gone and destroyed, you will know that state.

Do effort in treating this disease,

So that it does not place you again in the sorrows of birth.

In your own self, with its own vibrations, the ocean of consciousness shines.

Contemplate firmly that it is one, undivided.

That power of consciousness, slightly agitated in form,

Shines purely in the ocean of consciousness, like a wave in the ocean.

In the self, by the self, in space, like the wind in a lake,

Thus, the self, by its own power, attains movement in itself.

For a moment, that goddess shines with all powers.

Space, time, action, power do not limit her.

Having known her own nature, even though established in the infinite state,

She contemplates a limited form, though uncontemplated.

When such a form is contemplated by that supremely beloved,

Then, following her, the views of name, number, etc., arise.

This form of consciousness, imagined with imaginations, the abode of space, time, and action,

O Brahman, is called the knower of the field.

He, imagining desires, again attains egohood.

The ego, the determiner, tainted, is called intellect.

The intellect, with imagined forms, attains the state of mind.

The mind, with dense imaginations, slowly attains the state of senses.

The senses are known by the wise as the body made of hands and feet.

Thus, the individual, bound by the rope of imaginative desires,

Surrounded by the net of sorrows, gradually attains lowliness.

Thus, the mind, made of power, having attained dense egohood,

Like a silkworm, willingly goes into bondage.

Having itself imagined the net of subtle elements within,

It attains great helplessness, like a lion bound by chains.

Sometimes mind, sometimes intellect, sometimes knowledge, sometimes action,

Sometimes this ego, sometimes called mind.

Sometimes called nature, sometimes imagined as illusion,

Sometimes called dirt, sometimes remembered as action.

Sometimes known as bondage, sometimes remembered as the city of eight,

Called ignorance sometimes, accepted as desire sometimes.

This entire world, indicating the noose of desires,

Bears within, like a fig tree bearing figs, without fruits.

Burnt by the flame of thought, chewed by the serpent of anger,

Delighting in the waves of the ocean of desire, having forgotten the grandfather self,

Lift up the mind, O Brahman, like an elephant from mud.

Thus, the states dependent on the individual, caused by the contemplation of existence,

Imagined by Brahman in forms, in lakhs or crores,

Countless, born before, are born even now everywhere,

And will be born again, like streams of particles from a waterfall.

Some with first birth, some with more than a hundred births,

Some with countless births, some with two or three existences,

Some as Kinnaras, Gandharvas, Vidyadharas, great serpents,

Some as sun, moon, Varuna, three-eyed, lord of senses, Brahma,

Some as Brahmanas, kings, Vaishyas, Shudras,

Some as grass, herbs, trees, fruits, roots, insects,

Some as Kadamba, Jambira, Sala, Tala, Tamala trees,

Some as Mount Mahendra, Malaya, Sahya, Mandara, Meru,

Some as oceans of salt, milk, ghee, sugarcane juice,

Some as vast directions, some as rivers with great speed,

Some fly high in the sky, fall and rise again,

Like balls hit by death incessantly.

Having experienced thousands of births, again in the difficulty of the world,

Some ignorant ones fall, even after attaining discrimination.

The self-truth, unlimited by space, time, etc., through its own power,

Playfully assumes a body limited by space and time.

That itself, through the influence of individual desires, becomes the mind,

Unsteady, inclined to imagination and non-imagination.

The power of the mind first contemplates in a moment

The contemplation of space, pure, inclined to the taste of sound-seed.

Then, having attained density, through the sequence of dense movement, the mind

Contemplates the movement of wind, inclined to the taste of touch-seed.

From space and wind, through the force of firm practice,

From the forms of sound and touch, through friction, fire is born.

With the subtle element of form, measured with those three,

The mind attains that quality, the perception of taste-subtle element.

In a moment, it perceives the coolness of water, then the knowledge of water.

Then, attaining that quality, the mind contemplates in a moment

The subtle element of smell; from that, the knowledge of earth arises.

Then, enveloped by the subtle elements thus, abandoning subtlety,

It sees the body shining in space like a spark of fire,

Endowed with the imagination of ego, accompanied by the seed of intellect.

That is called the city of eight, the six-footed one in the heart-lotus of beings.

In that, through intense impulse, contemplating the luminous body,

It attains grossness through cooking, like the fruit of the Bilva tree.

Like melted gold in a crucible, shining in the pure sky,

Then, that light assumes a form by its own nature:

Above, the head-mass; below, the feet;

On the sides, the hands; in the middle, the belly.

In time, attaining clarity, it becomes a spotless body,

Established in intellect, goodness, strength, enthusiasm, knowledge, sovereignty.

He is the blessed Brahma, the grandfather of all worlds.

Seeing his own excellent, beautiful body, Brahma,

Attaining thought, the seer of the three times without impurity,

Thought, 'In this supreme space, consisting of pure consciousness alone,

Unseen, with unlimited ends, what was first?'

Thus, Brahma thought, and immediately, with a pure self-vision,

Saw the multitudes of creations that had passed before.

Then, he remembered all, with all qualities and sequences.

Playfully, he created various creatures from imagination,

With various conducts and beginnings, like a Gandharva city.

For their sake, for heaven and liberation, for the attainment of duty, desire, and wealth,

He created countless wonderful scriptures.

From the form of Brahma, since the world is created from the mind,

Its existence is said to last as long as that; with its destruction, it attains destruction.

Nothing is born, nothing dies, anywhere, ever.

In reality, O best of Brahmins, all is seen falsely.

Abandon the spectacle of the world, the snakes of desires in the sheath.

Knowing that this is unreal, establish the state of the mother.

For the decorated or undecorated Gandharva city,

In the part of ignorance, in son, etc., what is the order of happiness and sorrow?

In increased wealth and wives, there is no satisfaction in sorrow.

When the illusion of delusion increases, who is comforted here?

By whom attachment arises in the fool, by those very parts,

In the wise, detachment arises.

Therefore, O Nidagha, knower of truth, in the transactions of the world,

Neglect what is lost as lost, accept what is gained as gained.

The natural non-desire for future enjoyments,

And the enjoyment of what comes—these are the marks of the learned.

Having understood the state between existence and non-existence, relying on it,

Do not grasp the seen, external and internal; do not abandon it.

For the knower who acts with desire and without desire,

His wisdom is not tainted, like a lotus leaf by water.

If the splendor of sense objects does not stir in your heart, O twice-born,

Then you have known what is to be known, crossed the ocean of existence.

With supreme wisdom, from the multitude of desires,

Like extracting fragrance from a flower, separate the mind’s function.

In this ocean of the world, flooded with the water of desires,

Those who have boarded the ship of wisdom have crossed, the supreme learned ones.

They neither abandon nor desire the transactions of the world.

They follow everything, the knowers of the ocean.

Of the infinite self-truth, in the form of general existence,

The inclination of consciousness towards objects is known as the sprout of resolve.

Having attained a little existence, it slowly attains density,

Becomes mind, filling with inertia like a cloud.

Consciousness contemplates objects as if separate from itself,

Like a seed becoming a sprout.

Imagination itself gives birth to imagination.

It grows quickly by itself, for sorrow, not for happiness.

Do not imagine imagination; do not contemplate states in stability.

Being effortful in destroying imagination, it does not follow again.

By mere non-contemplation, imagination perishes by itself.

With imagination alone, cut the imagination; with the mind, the mind, O sage.

Having cut it, stay in the self; what is difficult in this?

Just as this space is empty, so is the world empty.

As the skin of rice, as the blackness of copper,

Perishes through action, so does the dirt of a person.

Like the dirt of the individual’s rice, though natural,

It certainly perishes; there is no doubt; therefore, be diligent."

Thus is the Mahopanishad.

End of the Fifth Chapter.

Chapter 6

"Having abandoned the inner attachment to the splendor of states, which is made of contemplation,

As you are, so you are; play in this world, O sinless one.

With the firm contemplation 'I am not the doer everywhere,'

That supreme nectar-named equanimity alone remains.

In the plays of sorrow, joy, and delight, with the sole doership of the self,

When one’s resolve perishes, equanimity alone remains.

That equanimity in all states is the supreme true state.

The mind established in that does not become a partaker of birth again.

Or, O sage, having abandoned all doership and non-doership,

Having drunk the mind, be steady as you are.

Abandon that which you abandon for the sake of stability and concentration.

The form of the mind’s imagination, light and darkness, etc.,

Desire and its agent, along with the movement of breath,

Having completely abandoned all with roots, be peaceful like space, with intellect calmed.

Having completely abandoned all rows of desires from the heart,

He who stays without anxiety is liberated, the supreme lord.

The seen and the to-be-seen are all seen; the ten directions are deluded by delusion.

For the knower who acts with reason, the world is like a cow’s footprint.

In the body, external and internal, below, above, and in the directions,

Here is the self, there is the self; there is no world made of non-self.

There is nothing where I am not, there is nothing that is not mine.

What else do I desire? All is pervaded by existence and consciousness.

All this is indeed Brahman; all this is the self spread out.

Abandon the delusion 'I am other, this is other,' O sinless one.

In that dense Brahman, eternal, imagined things do not exist.

There is no sorrow, no delusion, no old age, no birth.

What exists here, that alone exists; be always free from fever.

From experiencing what comes, from not desiring everywhere,

From abandoning taking and giving, be always free from fever.

He whose birth is final, O great-minded one,

Quickly enters the pure knowledge like a bee into a bamboo.

For those with detached minds, properly through their own association,

It is said: From the union of the seer and the seen, there is certainty of the bliss of perception.

We worship that self-arisen, motionless bliss.

Having abandoned the seer, seeing, and seen along with desire,

We worship the self, the appearance of the perception of seeing.

Always in the middle of the two, 'exists' and 'does not exist,'

We worship the self, the illuminator of lights.

Those who, abandoning the lord in the cave of the heart, go to another god,

Desire a jewel while abandoning the Kaustubha gem in their hand.

Repeatedly, with the rod of discrimination,

Kill the enemies of the senses, arisen or unarisen, like Indra killing mountains with his thunderbolt.

In the dream of the night of the world, in the empty delusion of the body,

All is indeed impure; the seen is the delusion of the world.

Afflicted by ignorance in childhood, struck by women in youth,

In the rest, afflicted by thoughts of family—what does the lowly man do?

Non-existence stands on the head of existence, unpleasantness on the head of the pleasant,

Sorrows on the head of happiness—what one thing shall I resort to?

For those whose blinking causes the destruction and creation of the world,

Such persons go; what is the counting of those like me?

The world alone is said to be the limit of sorrows.

How can happiness be attained by the body fallen in its midst?

'I am awakened, I am awakened; this mind is a wicked thief.

I shall kill it, for I have been stolen by the mind for a long time.'

Do not grieve for the rejectable; do not be devoted to the acceptable.

Having abandoned the views of rejectable and acceptable, be steady in what remains.

Desirelessness, fearlessness, eternity, equanimity, knowledge,

Desirelessness, inactivity, gentleness, freedom from imaginations,

Firmness, friendliness, contentment of mind, softness, gentle speech—

These dwell in the knower free from rejectable and acceptable, without desires.

Having cut with the sharp blade of intellect the net of desires spread like a hunter’s net,

The water of the world spread with the threads of thoughts,

O dear one, having cut it like a cloud with a gale, stay in that state.

Having cut the mind with the mind, like cutting a tree with an axe,

Having attained the pure state, be steady immediately.

Whether sitting, going, sleeping, waking, dwelling, flying, falling,

Having determined that all this is unreal, abandon it completely.

If you rely on the seen, then you are bound with consciousness.

If you abandon the seen, then you are without mind, liberated.

Meditating 'I am not, nor is this,' stay as the immovable mountain.

Between the self and the world, between the seer and the seen,

Be always contemplating the self called vision.

Having abandoned the taste and the taster, being in the middle of taste and taster,

Meditating on the mere tasting, become absorbed in the supreme self.

Relying on the supportless, be steady in the middle of the middle.

Those bound by a rope are liberated; those bound by desire are not liberated by anyone.

Therefore, O Nidagha, abandon desire through the abandonment of resolves.

Having cut this evil ego made of 'I'-ness with the blade of 'not-I'-ness,

In the state of being and becoming, be with all fears of beings calmed.

'I am of these objects, and these are my life.

Without them, I am nothing, and without me, they are not.'

Having abandoned this inner certainty, reflecting with the mind,

When 'I am not of the object, nor is the object mine' is contemplated,

With a cool inner intellect, performing actions playfully,

That abandonment of desires is indeed proclaimed, O Brahman.

Having made the destruction of desires with equanimity in all,

He who abandons the body without attachment is not to be led; that is the destruction of desires.

He who, having abandoned the desire born of ego,

Stays playfully, abandoning the object of meditation, is called the living liberated.

He who, having completely abandoned the root of imagination, desires,

Has attained peace, know him as liberated, the best of Brahmins.

These two have attained Brahman, these two are free from fever:

The renunciant and the yogi, controlled, know them as peaceful, O lord of sages.

He who has no praise or blame for desired or undesired things in his inner vision,

And moves like one in deep sleep, is called the living liberated.

He who neither rejoices nor grieves, free from reflections,

With views of joy, anger, fear, wrath, desire, miserliness.

The desire arising from external objects is called bound.

The desire free from all objects is called liberated.

The intense chain of desire, accompanied by prayer,

Know it as giving birth to sorrow and fear.

Abandoning that in all states, whether existing or not, always,

The great-minded one attains the supremely generous state.

Abandoning the states of bondage and liberation, of happiness and sorrow,

Of existence and non-existence, stay like an undisturbed great ocean.

O good one, four kinds of certainty arise in a person.

From head to toe, I am created by mother and father—

This is one certainty, O Brahman, for bondage, from seeing poison.

I am thinner than the tip of a hair, beyond all states—

This is the second certainty for liberation, arising in the good.

I am the self of all objects in the world, imperishable—

This third certainty is also said for liberation, O best of twice-born.

I or the entire world is always empty, equal to space—

This fourth certainty also gives the attainment of liberation.

Among these, the first is said to be with desire suitable for bondage.

The three pure desires are for the living liberated, playful.

The certainty 'I am all this,' O great-minded one,

Having taken it, the mind does not arise again for sorrow.

It is called void, nature, illusion, Brahman, knowledge,

Shiva, Purusha, Ishana, eternal, self.

Through the play of creating the world born of duality and non-duality,

The supreme self-power shines as non-duality alone.

Relying on the state beyond all, completely filled with one consciousness,

Without agitation, without a satisfied self, he does not sink in the world.

Always performing attained actions, seeing enemies and friends equally,

Free from desired and undesired, he neither grieves nor desires.

Speaking what is agreeable to all, when urged, with beautiful speech,

Knowing the intentions of beings, he does not sink in the world.

Having taken up the previous vision, delighting in the abandonment of the object of meditation,

Established in living liberation, play in the world free from fever.

Having completely abandoned all desires within, free from attachment, without desires,

With all external conduct, play in the world free from fever.

With artificial enthusiasm externally, without enthusiasm in the heart,

A doer externally, a non-doer internally, play in the world with pure intellect.

Having abandoned ego, at peace, with a mind like space,

Without the mark of impurity, play in the world with pure intellect.

Generous, with gentle conduct, following all conducts,

Internally abandoning attachment, externally as if with possessions,

Internally with detachment, externally as if desiring.

'This is a friend, this is not'—such calculation is for the small-minded.

For those with generous conduct, the whole earth is family.

Free from existence and non-existence, free from old age and death,

Rely on the state that is calm, without imaginations, without attachment.

This is the state of Brahman, pure, without desires, free from disease.

Having taken it, one who plays thus does not become deluded in difficulties.

Through detachment, through scriptures, through qualities like greatness,

For the sake of destroying resolves, one should oneself elevate the mind.

Through detachment, the mind attains fullness, following destruction.

It attains redness through desire, like a lake in autumn.

Again and again, performing the multitude of actions without taste for enjoyment,

O wise one, why are you not ashamed day after day?

Consciousness tainted by objects is bondage; in its liberation, liberation is said.

Consciousness without objects is indeed the self—this is the summary of all doctrines.

Having taken this certainty, see with intellect and wisdom.

You yourself will attain the blissful state of the self through the self.

I am consciousness, these worlds are consciousness, the directions are consciousness, these beings are consciousness.

Free from the seen and seeing, I am alone, pure in form.

Eternally arisen, without appearance, the seer, the witness, of the nature of consciousness.

The form of consciousness free from objects, the form of full light,

With all knowables calmed, I am great, mere consciousness.

With all resolves calmed, with all desires calmed,

Having gone to the state without imaginations, be established, O lord of sages."

Thus.

He who studies this Mahopanishad daily, a Brahmin,

Becomes a scholar if not a scholar, becomes initiated if not initiated.

He becomes purified by fire, purified by wind, purified by the moon,

Purified by truth, purified by all. He becomes known by all gods.

He becomes bathed in all sacred places. He becomes meditated upon by all gods.

He becomes one who has sacrificed with all rituals. The fruits of chanting sixty thousand Gayatris are attained.

The fruits of chanting a hundred thousand Itihasas and Puranas are attained.

Ten thousand Pranavas are chanted. He purifies the row up to the eyes.

He purifies up to the seventh generation. Thus says the blessed Hiranyagarbha.

By chanting this, one attains immortality—thus is the Upanishad.

End of the Sixth Chapter.

Closing Invocation

Om, may my limbs, speech, vital force, eyes, ears, strength, and all senses be nourished.

All is the Brahman of the Upanishads. May I not reject Brahman, and may Brahman not reject me.

Let there be no rejection, let there be no rejection for me.

May the virtues in the Upanishads reside in me, who am devoted to the self.

May they reside in me.

Om, peace, peace, peace.

Thus ends the Mahopanishad.
